# Sleep hygiene — evidence-based

Reference for when the user asks "what should I do differently". Use these as experiments to suggest, not rules to enforce.

## High-leverage habits (strong evidence)

1. **Consistent wake time, even on weekends.** Probably the single highest-leverage habit. Keeps circadian rhythm aligned.
2. **Morning daylight within 30-60 min of waking**, ideally 10+ minutes outdoors. Anchors the circadian clock.
3. **Caffeine cutoff 8-10 hours before bed.** Half-life of caffeine is ~5-6 hours. Afternoon coffee affects sleep architecture even if falling asleep feels fine.
4. **Cool, dark room.** Around 18°C is commonly recommended; darker is better. Blackout curtains or eye mask help if morning light leaks in.
5. **Consistent bedtime routine** (even 15-20 min of winding down). The brain learns the pattern.

## Moderate-leverage habits

6. **No alcohol within 3 hours of bed.** Alcohol helps falling asleep but wrecks REM and causes wake-ups in the second half of the night.
7. **Last heavy meal 2-3 hours before bed.** Digestion raises core temperature and can disrupt deep sleep.
8. **No intense exercise within 1-2 hours of bed.** Morning or afternoon is fine; hard sessions close to bedtime delay sleep onset for many people.
9. **Dim lights 1-2 hours before bed.** Bright light in the evening suppresses melatonin.

## Weaker / controversial

- **Blue light glasses / night mode on screens** — evidence is mixed. Dimming total brightness matters more than filtering blue specifically.
- **Magnesium, melatonin, herbal teas** — melatonin can help with jet lag or shift work; for regular insomnia, evidence is modest. Don't recommend dosing; defer to a doctor.
- **Sleep trackers** — useful for patterns, not reliable for stage-by-stage accuracy on wrist-worn devices. "Sleep scores" are proprietary algorithms, not clinical measures.
- **Cold showers, breathwork, specific mattress brands** — individual response varies enormously. Worth trying, not worth promising.

## Common troubleshooting

### "I take a long time to fall asleep"

Likely causes, in order of frequency:
1. Going to bed too early for actual sleep drive.
2. Late caffeine or screen use.
3. Rumination / stress — consider writing down worries before bed.
4. Irregular schedule — body doesn't know it's bedtime.

Suggest one experiment at a time, not all of them.

### "I wake up at 3-4am and can't fall back asleep"

Classic middle-of-night awakening pattern. Common causes:
1. Alcohol the night before.
2. Blood sugar drop (esp. if dinner was early and carb-heavy).
3. Cortisol rising too early — can relate to chronic stress.
4. Room too warm.

If it's recurring, worth mentioning to a doctor to rule out other causes.

### "I sleep a lot but don't feel rested"

1. Quality over quantity — consistency might be off even if duration is fine.
2. Possible undiagnosed issue (apnea, if snoring + daytime fatigue).
3. Depression/anxiety can present as unrefreshing sleep.
4. Worth a medical check if it persists.

## What the research actually says about duration

- Most adults need 7-9 hours. Individual variation is real.
- Consistency matters more than hitting a number. Sleeping 7h at the same time every night is usually better than averaging 8h with a 2-hour variance.
- "Catching up" on weekends partially works for short-term debt but doesn't fully reverse chronic short sleep.
